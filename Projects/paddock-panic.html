About

Paddock Panic is a hyper-casual, isometric herding game where players take on the role of a farm dog tasked with guiding lost flocks of sheep back to their paddock before nightfall. Using simple but expressive controls, player draw paths to direct the dog's movement and bark to influence sheep and scare off threats.

Set in a deceptively peaceful farmland filled with cliffs, hazards, and stalking wolves, the game blends calm visuals with increasing urgency as time runs out. Players must balance speed, strategy, and multitasking, choosing whether to risk pursuing bonuses and power-ups or focus on safely escorting the sheep home. Even saving a single sheep counts as success, encouraging risk-reward decision-making and replayability to earn the best score.

My Role

Paddock Panic was developed over 2.5 months as the final project of my first year at AIE. Each team was tasked with creating a hyper-casual mobile-oriented game, with the option to develop for non-mobile platforms. Our team initially consisted of six members, but one programmer left early in development, leaving me as the sole programming student alongside two designers and two artists. This created technical challenges and required careful rescoping and prioritisation. Fortunately, both designers had prior experience working in Unreal Engine and contributed to areas such as hazards, UI, and scoring systems. The project was a valuable learning experience that strengthened my ability to collaborate across disciplines, adapt to shifting team dynamics, and deliver complex systems under constraints.

  • Main Game Loop Logic

    I implemented the core game loop that determines level flow, win and loss conditions, time pressure, and scoring. This included tracking sheep states, handling level completion based on remaining sheep and time constraints, and managing transitions between gameplay states such as active play, failure, and success.

  • AI, Behaviour Trees, and Flocking

    I developed the sheep and wolf AI using Unreal Engine's Behaviour Trees and Blackboard systems, with a strong focus on flocking and reactive behaviour. Sheep operate across multiple states (individual, grouped, and herded), transitioning dynamically based on proximity to the dog, nearby sheep, and active threats. Flocking behaviour was implemented using cohesion, alignment, and separation rules, allowing sheep to move naturally as a group while still reacting to danger. I also implemented wolf AI with stalking, hunting, and retreat states, enabling interactions where the player must actively intervene to protect the flock.

  • Player Movement

    I designed and implemented the player control system that allows the dog to be ordered to move through click-and-drag path drawing. This system records player input in real time, converts it into spline-based movement, and visually represents paths using particle effects for clarity and feedback. The movement system was built to be intuitive, responsive, and readable from an isometric perspective, encouraging players to plan routes around hazards.

  • Visual Effects (VFX) and Art

    I contributed to visual effects and early art implementation to support gameplay readability and player feedback. This included path-drawing effects, indicators, hazard feedback, and visual cues for sheep AI states such as flocking, panicing, and dying. These effects were designed to be clear and minimal, reinforcing the hyper-casual aesthetic while ensuring players could quickly understand cause-and-effect relationships during chaotic moments.
